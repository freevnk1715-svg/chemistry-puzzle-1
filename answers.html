7. Explanations

  1. 1 Down — CATHODE: Positive terminal in a galvanic cell but negative terminal in an electrolytic cell; reduction always occurs here regardless of polarity.
  2. 2 Across — GALVANIC: Also called a voltaic cell; the spontaneous cell reaction (negative Gibbs energy) drives electron flow through the external circuit.
  3. 3 Down — ANODE: Negative terminal in a galvanic cell but positive terminal in an electrolytic cell; oxidation always occurs here regardless of polarity.
  4. 4 Across — ZENER: Its sharp, reproducible reverse breakdown voltage is used to hold a constant output voltage in regulator circuits.
  5. 5 Down — RECTIFIER: Relies on the diode's unidirectional conduction: it conducts in forward bias and blocks current in reverse bias.
  6. 6 Down — REDUCTION: Occurs at the cathode in every electrochemical cell; e.g., Cu2+ gains two electrons to form Cu metal.
  7. 7 Down — SALTBRIDGE: Typically an inert electrolyte (e.g., KCl or KNO3) set in agar gel; it completes the internal circuit and minimises liquid-junction potential.
  8. 8 Across — DOPANT: Group 13 (trivalent) or Group 15 (pentavalent) atoms substituted into the Si/Ge lattice; creates acceptor or donor energy levels.
  9. 9 Across — PHOTODIODE: Photons striking the junction generate extra electron-hole pairs, increasing the reverse saturation current in proportion to light intensity.
  10. 10 Down — ELECTROLYTIC: An externally applied EMF, larger than the cell's back EMF, drives ions to the electrodes and brings about electrolysis.
  11. 11 Across — PTYPE: Trivalent dopant creates an electron deficiency (hole); acceptor level lies just above the valence band.
  12. 12 Across — LANTHANIDE: Caused by the poor shielding ability of 4f electrons, producing a steady, cumulative fall in size across the series.
  13. 13 Across — IONICRADIUS: Losing electrons reduces electron-electron repulsion (cation shrinks); gaining electrons increases it (anion expands).
  14. 14 Down — NTYPE: Pentavalent dopant contributes one extra (donor) electron per atom; donor level lies just below the conduction band.
  15. 15 Down — TRANSISTOR: An n-p-n or p-n-p sandwich with a thin, lightly-doped base region that allows small base current to control large collector current.
  16. 16 Down — DIAGONAL: Arises from comparable polarizing power; classic pairs are Li-Mg and Be-Al, which show similar chemical behaviour.
  17. 17 Down — METALLIC: Linked to low ionization enthalpy and electropositive behaviour; most metallic elements sit toward the lower-left of the periodic table.
  18. 18 Down — ATOMICRADIUS: Governed by increasing effective nuclear charge across a period and the addition of new shells down a group.
  19. 19 Across — ELECTRONEGATIVE: Fluorine is the most such element on the Pauling scale; the tendency broadly increases across a period and decreases down a group.
  20. 20 Down — ELECTROLYSIS: Forms the experimental basis of Faraday's laws of electrolysis; used industrially in electroplating and the extraction/refining of metals.
  21. 21 Down — EXTRINSIC: Doping a pure (intrinsic) semiconductor converts it into this type, giving unequal electron and hole concentrations (n-type or p-type).
  22. 22 Down — BANDGAP: Near zero/overlapping in conductors, about 1 eV in semiconductors, and more than 3 eV in insulators.
  23. 23 Across — FUELCELL: Reactant gases (commonly H2 and O2) are fed in continuously; valued for high efficiency and pollution-free operation, e.g., in spacecraft.
  24. 24 Across — INTRINSIC: A pure Si/Ge crystal with no added impurity; electrons and holes are produced in equal numbers solely by breaking of covalent bonds on heating.
  25. 25 Down — IONIZATION: Generally rises across a period and falls down a group, with notable irregularities at half-filled and fully-filled subshells.
  26. 26 Down — GROUP: There are 18 such columns; elements within one show closely similar chemical properties.
  27. 27 Down — PERIOD: There are 7 such rows; properties change progressively across a row owing to increasing nuclear charge.
  28. 28 Across — CORROSION: Occurs because tiny galvanic cells are set up on the metal surface itself, often driven by differential aeration.
  29. 29 Across — OXIDATION: Occurs at the anode in every electrochemical cell; e.g., Zn is converted to Zn2+ by losing two electrons.
  30. 30 Across — SHIELDING: Also called the screening effect; for a given shell, s-electrons shield more effectively than p, d or f electrons.
